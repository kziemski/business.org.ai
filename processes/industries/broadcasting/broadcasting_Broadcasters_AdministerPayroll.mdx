## Industry Variations

Payroll administration in broadcasting involves distinct complexities compared to standard industries:

- **Union considerations**: Significant portions of broadcast talent and crew are covered by SAG-AFTRA, IATSE, WGA, and DGA agreements with specific pay scales, residuals, and benefit requirements
- **Talent residuals**: Ongoing payments for reruns, syndication, and streaming require specialized tracking and payment systems
- **Variable compensation**: On-air talent often receives ratings-based bonuses, appearance fees, and endorsement-related compensation requiring complex calculations
- **Multi-state taxation**: National broadcasts and remote productions create nexus in multiple jurisdictions, complicating payroll tax compliance
- **Freelance workforce**: Heavy reliance on freelance camera operators, editors, and production crew requires 1099 processing alongside W-2 employees
- **Per diem and travel**: Location shoots and remote broadcasts involve substantial per diem, meal penalties, and travel reimbursements governed by union rules
